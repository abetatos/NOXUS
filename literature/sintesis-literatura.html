Findings (technical)
  • Dynamic panel-data framework using OMI tropospheric NO₂ as an independent activity signal to externally audit reported GDP, exploiting both cross-section and time-series dimensions.
  • Over the Great Recession window, NO₂ implied a sharp activity drop (−7.8%) while reported GDP (+22.3%) and electricity (+5.5%) did not — NO₂ flags the divergence.

  • Direct precedent for the "NO₂ as audit on Chinese output" thesis; we cite it as the parent and state the delta: single cluster (not national), TROPOMI (not OMI), leading-indicator framing (not retrospective GDP validation).

  • OMI is coarser and older than TROPOMI; national scale hides the cluster signal we need.

Findings (technical)

Findings (technical)
  • Unsupervised clustering of NO₂ columns + a convolutional Getis-Ord Gi* (ConvGi) local-autocorrelation statistic separates the shipping signal from background. Spatial correlation with ship-traffic frequency rises from 0.448 (raw) to 0.624 (clustered+ConvGi) in the Mediterranean (p<0.01).
  • Temporal validation against the RWI/ISL Container Throughput Index: seasonally-adjusted NO₂ slant column (SCD) gives r = 0.71 (Red Sea), 0.56 (Med.), 0.55 (Indian); raw VCD is insignificant or negative — the choice of SCD and deseasonalization is decisive.

Use for NOXUS
  • Our direct blueprint: cluster TROPOMI over a region → isolate the source from background → correlate against a sector throughput index. We swap shipping/containers for steel/crude-steel.
  • Concrete lessons: prefer relative signal; deseasonalize before correlating; report r with p-values; expect r in the 0.5–0.7 band for a clean sector link (a quantitative bar for our own result).
Data & code
NO₂: TROPOMI L2 via Copernicus S5P hub, v1.2.0/1.2.2 (regridded).

Problems / caveats
  • VCD sensitive to a-priori NO₂ profile shape; relative hotspots only, no absolute emission.
  • Spatial r is moderate (~0.5–0.6) even after processing — ceiling on attribution skill.

Findings (technical)
  • NOₓ point sources from the divergence of the horizontal NO₂ flux (flux = TVCD × wind); divergence isolates the local source term from background, highly sensitive to point sources.
  • 451 automatically detected sources incl. metal smelters, cement plants, industrial areas; localization 2–3 km.
  • Emissions biased low by a factor 2–8, driven mainly by underestimation of near-surface NO₂ in the TROPOMI column.
Use for NOXUS
  • The core attribution method — and proof it already resolves steel-type industrial emitters, justifying source-level attribution over Tangshan.
  • The low bias is the formal reason to work with a relative index, not absolute tonnes, and to restrict to large isolated emitters.
Data & code
NO₂: TROPOMI TVCD.
Catalog (open): DOI 10.26050/WDCC/Quant_NOx_TROPOMI.
Method: flux-divergence; fully automated peak detection.
Problems / caveats
  • Incomplete at coastlines, complex/mountainous winds, and high-background polluted regions (noisy divergence) — all relevant to industrial NE China.
  • Area sources (megacities) skipped; co-located traffic/heating contaminate the steel term.

Findings (technical)
  • Two-way FE panel + country trends: NO₂ retains significant explanatory power for GDP jointly with night lights, CO₂, electricity and survey data (coef 0.11***); lights alone predict annual fluctuations in only ~20% of countries.
  • Growth elasticity of NO₂ ≈ 0.35, found constant across nations via an errors-in-variables identification exploiting that NO₂ (day) and lights (night) have orthogonal measurement errors.
  • Rebuts China's GDP; quantifies under-reporting elsewhere (France/UK/Spain 2020 GDP under-reported by 76/181/205 bps). Combustion ≈ 84% of world energy.
  • Their own literature search found only two prior studies quantifying NO₂ vs official economic measures (Montgomery 2018; Wei/Kondragunta) — explicit evidence of the gap.
Use for NOXUS
  • Cite for (a) NO₂ > lights where data are manipulated (our China steel-data-integrity motivation), (b) timeliness (published within days → leading), (c) the thin-literature gap.
  • β≈0.35 is a national/annual elasticity — our contribution is the cluster/sub-annual regime it does not cover.
Data & code
NO₂: NASA daily global product (OMI-class).
Lights: DMSP-OLS (pre-2014) + VIIRS (2014–20).
Output: national accounts; 1,900+ household surveys (overident. checks).
Problems / caveats
  • NO₂ captures combustion-linked activity only; weak for long-run trend in decarbonizing rich economies.
  • National/annual scope — does not address sectoral attribution or sub-annual lead/lag.

Synthesis

Global findings

What is proven, the shared traps with their measured magnitudes, the toolbox, and the quantitative bar our own result has to clear.

One-sentence thesis

Every link is independently proven — NO₂ as a manipulation-resistant activity proxy (β≈0.35 elasticity, NO₂ flags GDP/electricity divergences), flux-divergence attribution to industrial point sources (v2 within ±20% of inventories), and validating a sector against a throughput index (r≈0.55–0.75) — but no one has chained them into a public, reproducible, single-cluster pipeline that reports the null. That intersection is NOXUS.

Proven (green light)

  • NO₂↔activity is strong where combustion dominates: r=0.68–0.75 (US traffic/unemployment), elasticity β≈0.35 across 178 countries KondraguntaEzran.
  • Sector throughput recoverable from clustered NO₂: r≈0.55–0.71 on deseasonalized series Kim.
  • Industrial attribution works and is now accurate to ±20% Beirle v2; a sector activity shock is invertible Mao −34%.
  • Expected real-shock magnitude: NOₓ −36% (China COVID), with Hebei in focus Zheng.

The gap NOXUS fills

  • Single cluster (Tangshan), not national vs Morris&Zhangvs Ezran.
  • Public + reproducible end-to-end, vs closed commercial indices.
  • TROPOMI (3.5×5.6 km) + sub-annual / leading framing.
  • Validation against physical output (crude steel, BF rate), not PMI.
  • Commitment to report the null; literature is explicitly thin (only 2 prior NO₂↔official-activity studies per Ezran).

Five shared traps — with measured magnitudes

  • 1 · Policy confounder. Controls cut NOₓ while output rises (China 2021–22); Tangshan steel's NO₂ share itself drops 42.5%→29.1% in the heating season Li 2024SciRep steel.
  • 2 · Non-universal, non-fixed sign. Positive in only 38/56 low-income cities, flat in middle-income — verify the sign for Tangshan Montgomery&Holloway.
  • 3 · Spatial scale. At 0.1° coefficients lose significance and flip vs 0.25° — tune and report scale sensitivity Parubets&Naito.
  • 4 · Cloud gaps + incomplete attribution. Coverage 0.36 by area (0.82 pop-weighted), non-random; divergence fails at coasts/complex winds/high background Li&ZhengBeirle.
  • 5 · Bias + source mixing. TROPOMI underestimates 23–33% in polluted air → use relative signal; steel is only ~30–43% of Tangshan NO₂ → non-linear pixel→tonnage KondraguntaSciRep steel.

Quantitative bar for our result

A credible steel signal should land in the precedents' range: r ≈ 0.5–0.75 vs crude-steel output on a deseasonalized series; a real production swing should move NOₓ on the order of tens of percent (cf. −36% COVID, −34% Ukraine industry); and the attributable steel fraction (~30–43%) caps how much of the column we can claim. Below that band, with policy and season controlled, the honest call is a null.
